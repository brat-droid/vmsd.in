Conflict Resolution

Conflict is an inevitable part of any project. When you bring together a group of passionate and talented individuals with different perspectives and priorities, disagreements are bound to arise. While conflict can be uncomfortable, it is not necessarily a bad thing. When managed constructively, conflict can lead to better ideas, stronger relationships, and more innovative solutions. The key is to have a clear and effective process for conflict resolution. This article will explore the common sources of conflict in projects and provide a framework for resolving it in a positive and productive way.

Common Sources of Conflict in Projects

Conflict in projects can arise from a variety of sources. Understanding the root cause of a conflict is the first step towards resolving it. Some of the most common sources of conflict include:

  • Scarce Resources: Competition for limited resources, such as budget, equipment, or personnel, is a classic source of conflict.
  • Scheduling Priorities: Disagreements about the sequence of activities, the duration of tasks, and the overall project timeline are common.
  • Personal Work Styles: People have different ways of working. A clash of personalities or work styles can lead to friction and conflict.
  • Differing Technical Opinions: In technical projects, there can be strong disagreements about the best way to solve a problem.
  • Unclear Roles and Responsibilities: When people don't have a clear understanding of who is responsible for what, it can lead to confusion, duplication of effort, and conflict.
  • Stress: The high-pressure environment of many projects can shorten tempers and make conflict more likely.

The Thomas-Kilmann Conflict Mode Instrument (TKI)

One of the most widely used models for understanding conflict resolution styles is the Thomas-Kilmann Conflict Mode Instrument (TKI). The TKI identifies five different styles of conflict resolution, based on two dimensions: assertiveness (the extent to which you try to satisfy your own concerns) and cooperativeness (the extent to which you try to satisfy the other person's concerns).

1. Competing (High Assertiveness, Low Cooperativeness)

This is the "win-lose" style. People who use this style are assertive and uncooperative. They are focused on their own goals and are not afraid to push for them, even at the expense of others. This style can be useful in situations where a quick, decisive action is needed, but it can also damage relationships.

2. Accommodating (Low Assertiveness, High Cooperativeness)

This is the "lose-win" style. People who use this style are unassertive and cooperative. They are willing to sacrifice their own needs to satisfy the needs of others. This style can be useful for preserving relationships, but it can also lead to resentment if one person is always giving in.

3. Avoiding (Low Assertiveness, Low Cooperativeness)

This is the "lose-lose" style. People who use this style are unassertive and uncooperative. They try to avoid conflict altogether by sidestepping the issue or withdrawing from the situation. This style can be useful for trivial issues, but it can also allow problems to fester and grow.

4. Collaborating (High Assertiveness, High Cooperativeness)

This is the "win-win" style. People who use this style are both assertive and cooperative. They work with the other person to find a solution that fully satisfies both of their concerns. This is often the most effective style of conflict resolution, but it can also be the most time-consuming.

5. Compromising (Moderate Assertiveness, Moderate Cooperativeness)

This is the "split the difference" style. People who use this style are moderately assertive and cooperative. They look for a solution that is partially satisfying to both parties. This style can be useful for finding a quick, middle-ground solution, but it may not be the most optimal solution for either party.

A Practical Guide to Resolving Conflict

While understanding the different conflict styles is useful, you also need a practical process for resolving conflict when it arises. Here is a simple, five-step process you can use:

  1. Acknowledge the conflict: The first step is to acknowledge that a conflict exists. Don't ignore it or hope that it will go away on its own.
  2. Define the problem: Work with the parties involved to define the problem in a clear and objective way. Focus on the issue, not the personalities.
  3. Generate solutions: Brainstorm a list of potential solutions to the problem. Encourage creativity and don't judge any ideas at this stage.
  4. Choose a solution: Evaluate the potential solutions and choose the one that best meets the needs of all parties.
  5. Implement the solution and follow up: Once you have chosen a solution, you need to implement it and then follow up to make sure that it is working.

Conclusion

Conflict is a normal and healthy part of any project. The key is to manage it in a way that is constructive, not destructive. By understanding the sources of conflict, by being aware of your own conflict resolution style, and by following a structured process for resolving disagreements, you can turn conflict into an opportunity for growth and innovation. A project manager who is skilled in conflict resolution is a valuable asset to any team and is far more likely to lead their projects to success.
