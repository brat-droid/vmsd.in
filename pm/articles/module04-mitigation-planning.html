Mitigation Planning

Identifying and analyzing risks is only half the battle. The real test of a good risk management process is what you do about them. This is where mitigation planning comes in. Mitigation planning is the process of developing options and actions to enhance opportunities and to reduce threats to project objectives. It's about being proactive, not reactive. Instead of waiting for a risk to happen, you develop a plan to deal with it before it becomes a problem.

Strategies for Responding to Negative Risks (Threats)

When it comes to negative risks, there are four basic strategies you can use. The right strategy will depend on the specific risk and your organization's risk tolerance.

1. Avoid

Risk avoidance is the strategy of changing the project plan to eliminate the risk or to protect the project objectives from its impact. This could involve changing the scope, extending the schedule, or using a different technology. In extreme cases, it could even mean canceling the project altogether. Avoidance is the most effective strategy, as it completely eliminates the risk, but it's not always possible or practical.

2. Transfer

Risk transfer is the strategy of shifting the impact of a risk to a third party, together with ownership of the response. This is most effective for financial risks. The most common form of risk transfer is insurance. You can also transfer risk through contracts, warranties, and performance bonds. Transferring a risk doesn't eliminate it, but it does give you a way to manage the financial consequences if the risk occurs.

3. Mitigate

Risk mitigation is the most common risk response strategy. It involves taking steps to reduce the probability of a risk occurring or the impact it would have if it did occur. Examples of mitigation include implementing a new quality control process, creating a more detailed project plan, or providing additional training to the project team. The goal of mitigation is to reduce the risk to an acceptable level.

4. Accept

Risk acceptance is the strategy of acknowledging a risk and not taking any action to address it. This strategy is appropriate for low-priority risks that have a low probability of occurring and a low potential impact. Acceptance can be either active or passive. Active acceptance involves developing a contingency plan in case the risk occurs. Passive acceptance involves doing nothing and simply dealing with the consequences if the risk occurs.

Strategies for Responding to Positive Risks (Opportunities)

Just as you have strategies for dealing with threats, you also have strategies for dealing with opportunities. These strategies are designed to increase the probability of a positive risk occurring or to enhance its impact.

  • Exploit: This is the most aggressive strategy for positive risks. It involves taking action to ensure that the opportunity is realized. For example, if you have an opportunity to finish a project early, you might assign your best resources to the critical path tasks to make sure it happens.
  • Enhance: This strategy involves taking steps to increase the probability of the opportunity occurring or to enhance its impact. For example, if there is a chance that a new technology could speed up your project, you might invest in a prototype to test it out.
  • Share: This strategy involves sharing the opportunity with a third party who is better able to capture the benefit for the project. This is often done through partnerships or joint ventures.
  • Accept: Just as with negative risks, you can also choose to accept a positive risk. This means that you don't take any special action to pursue the opportunity, but you are ready to take advantage of it if it happens to occur.

The Risk Response Plan

The output of the mitigation planning process is the risk response plan. This plan should be a part of your overall project management plan. The risk response plan should document all of the risks you have identified, your assessment of their probability and impact, and the specific actions you will take to respond to each risk. For each risk, the plan should include:

  • The identified risk and its description.
  • The chosen risk response strategy.
  • The specific actions to be taken to implement the strategy.
  • The person or team responsible for implementing the response.
  • The budget and schedule for the response.
  • Any contingency plans in case the primary response is not effective.

Conclusion

Mitigation planning is where the rubber meets the road in risk management. It's the process of turning your analysis into action. By developing a comprehensive risk response plan, you can move from being a passive victim of circumstance to being an active manager of uncertainty. A well-thought-out mitigation plan will not only help you to avoid threats, but it will also enable you to seize opportunities. It is a key ingredient for increasing the predictability of your projects and for ensuring that you deliver on your commitments to your stakeholders.
